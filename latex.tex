\begin{abstract}
Predicting residential electricity consumption at meter level is a challenging time-series problem
since household demand is shaped by multiple factors such as long-range weekly rhythms as well as transient events. In this paper we present a comparative study of four Temporal
Fusion Transformer model variants for residential energy consumption forecasting.
Starting from a baseline TFT model with engineered calendar and power features (V1), we improved the input space using Principle Component Analysis technique i.e. PCA(V2), where we retained 12 feature components only. We also trained a TFT model which made use of both PCA as well as Bayesian Hyperparameter search for hyperparameter tuning(V3). We then proceeded to improve
the input space with Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) sub band statistics and combined them with PCA in the final model (V4). Under the same full-dataset GPU conditions as V1, V4
surpasses the baseline: test MAE 78.57, RMSE 257.02, R$^2$ 0.8674, MAPE
23.58\% versus the baseline's 79.19, 259.44, 0.8649, and 27.05\%. The MAPE improvement of
12.8\% is the most pronounced gain. If we compare with the prior LSTM-SVR study conducted on the
UCI Steel Industry dataset \cite{reddy2022stacking}, TFT is better suited to residential forecasting since it is using the attention mechanism.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
 
Predicting electricity consumption at the household level is a major part for making smart grids and renewable energy integration actually work. Unlike industrial loads, which are usually steady, residential data is messy. It's shaped by human behavior such as daily routines, weekly rhythms, and random spikes from heavy appliances, making it a challenging signal to model.

Traditional methods like ARIMA usually struggle here because residential data is not linear or steady only. In a previous part of this project series, SVR and LSTM models were tested on industrial steel consumption data \cite{reddy2022stacking}, where SVR performed well due to the smaller, smoother dataset. However, SVR does not scale well when you have to handle multiple meters at once, which is the reality for most residential setups.

The Temporal Fusion Transformer (TFT) \cite{lim2021tft} is designed specifically for this kind of multi-horizon forecasting. It handles different types of inputs like calendar data which we know in advance and power measurements which we do not and uses an attention mechanism to figure out which features actually matter for the prediction.

To push the performance further, we can look at frequency-domain features using Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT). Wavelets are good at breaking down a signal into sub-bands, separating long-term trends from sudden peaks. We can also use Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to clean up redundancy in the data and Bayesian optimization (via Optuna) so we stop guessing hyperparameters and start tuning them systematically.

This paper does three things only. First, we build a full TFT pipeline for the THC residential dataset. Second, we test four model versions: a baseline (V1), a version with PCA (V2), a hyperparameter-tuned version using Optuna (V3), and a final version combining wavelets and PCA (V4). Finally, we break down the results and compare them to the previous LSTM-SVR study \cite{reddy2022stacking} to see where TFT actually adds value.

\section{Related Work}
 
\subsection{LSTM and SVR Baselines}
The prior study in this project series had evaluated LSTM networks and SVR on the UCI Steel
Industry Energy Consumption dataset \cite{reddy2022stacking}. SVR with RBF kernel and
feature scaling substantially outperformed all LSTM variants (SVR R$^2$ = 0.9980, best
LSTM R$^2$ = 0.5425). The gap was attributed to the moderate dataset size (35,040
samples) and the smooth, low-dimensional nature of industrial load profiles, which kernel
methods capture well without deep sequential modelling.
 
\subsection{Temporal Fusion Transformers}
Lim~et~al.\ \cite{lim2021tft} proposed TFT as a hybrid joining together recurrent layers for
local context with self-attention for long term dependencies. Its explicit partitioning of
static, known future, and unknown future inputs makes it naturally suited to metered
energy data where calendar features are available already ahead of forecast time.
 
\subsection{Wavelet-Enhanced Forecasting}
Discrete wavelet decomposition has been combined with neural networks in energy
forecasting to provide frequency localised context. The DWT separates a signal into an
approximation sub band and multiple detail sub bands at progressively finer scales.
Per meter sub band statistics, when appended as features, enrich the model without changing
the architecture.

\section{Dataset and Preprocessing}
 
\subsection{THC Residential Consumption Dataset}
Experiments use a multi meter residential dataset collected at minute level resolution
spanning August 2019 to October 2020 from Uruguay, with eleven monthly CSV files concatenated
and aligned on a common datetime index. Each row contain per-phase active power
(\texttt{apower\_ph1}), reactive power, phase voltage, current, power factor, and
cumulative energy for one meter reading. After concatenation the dataset contains
approximately 144,256 rows across multiple meter identifiers. Missing values were
imputed meter wise using forward-fill followed by backward-fill. Redundant phase-2
and phase-3 energy columns were dropped.
 
\subsection{Feature Engineering}
A shared base feature set is built same for all three variants: apparent power
($S = V \cdot I$), power factor angle ($\theta = \arccos(\text{pf})$), energy intensity,
reactive-to-active power ratio ($Q/P$), hour-of-day, day-of-week, binary weekend and
peak-hours (08:00--20:00) flags, and a three-step rolling mean of active power.
 
\subsection{Train / Validation / Test Split}
Data is split chronologically per meter to prevent leakage: 70\% training,
up to 85\% validation (cumulative), full series as test. This yields approximately
100,977, 122,615 and 144,256 samples respectively only.

\subsection{V1: Baseline TFT}

V1 takes the 18-feature engineered set as input with no further transformation. The feature set consist of nine raw electrical measurements (active energy total and per phase, reactive energy total and per phase, active power, reactive power, current, power factor, voltage) and five derived quantities: apparent power, power factor angle, energy intensity, reactive-to-active power ratio, and a 3-step rolling mean of active power. Four binary calendar flags round out the set: hour of day, day of week, weekend indicator, and peak-hours indicator (08:00--20:00).

Inside TFT, these are split into known-real inputs (time index plus the four calendar flags) and unknown-real inputs (the nine measurements and five derived features). The architecture uses hidden size 32, four attention heads, dropout 0.1, and hidden-continuous size 16. Output is 7-quantile via \texttt{QuantileLoss}. Each meter is normalised separately using \texttt{GroupNormalizer}. The encoder window is 24 timesteps (12 hours of 30-minute readings). Training used the Adam optimiser at $10^{-3}$, gradient clipping 0.1, early stopping with patience 5, and a cap of 15 epochs. The model has 115.5k trainable parameters. V1 ran on a Kaggle T4$\times$2 instance; the Lightning trainer used one of the two available GPUs.

\subsection{V2: PCA-Enhanced TFT}

V2 keeps the same TFT architecture and training setup as V1, adding 12 PCA components computed from the 14 continuous base features (all features except the four binary calendar flags). The scaler and PCA transform are fitted on the training partition only. Validation and test partitions are projected using that same fitted transform, so no future information is entering the earlier splits.

The 12 PCA components are appended to the 18 original features, giving 30 time-varying inputs in total. The motivation was that several base features are strongly correlated, for example apparent power, active power, and current move together closely, and PCA projections should help TFT's variable selection network by decorrelating the redundant directions. V2 ran on a single local GPU.

\subsection{V3: PCA with Bayesian Hyperparameter Search}

V3 starts from the same 30-feature set as V2 and adds Optuna-driven hyperparameter search. The search space is: learning rate in $[10^{-4},\,10^{-2}]$ (log-uniform), hidden size in $\{16, 32, 64\}$, attention head count in $\{1, 4\}$, dropout in $[0.1, 0.3]$, and hidden-continuous size in $\{8, 16, 32\}$. Each trial was running up to 15 epochs with early stopping; Optuna minimised the best validation loss seen during the trial.

The study selected: learning rate $8.03 \times 10^{-3}$, hidden size 16, one attention head, dropout 0.195, hidden-continuous size 16. That gives roughly 89k parameters, well below V1's 115.5k. The consequences of this choice is discussed in Section~\ref{sec:results}. V3 ran on the same local GPU as V2.

\subsection{V4: Wavelet plus PCA TFT}

V4 combines wavelet features and PCA under the same training conditions as V1. It was run on a Kaggle T4$\times$2 instance (one GPU used) on the full 144,256-row dataset across all 14 meters, with the same 15-epoch budget.

After extracting DWT sub-band statistics, PCA is fit on all continuous wavelet-augmented features using only the training partition. Twelve principal components are retained, explaining 97.59\% of the combined feature variance. Two cyclical time encodings are added to the known-real set:

\begin{equation}
h_{\sin} = \sin\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi \cdot \mathrm{hour}}{24}\right), \quad
h_{\cos} = \cos\!\left(\tfrac{2\pi \cdot \mathrm{hour}}{24}\right)
\end{equation}

Raw integer hour (0--23) creates a discontinuity between hour 23 and hour 0 in feature space. The sine/cosine pair removes this by wrapping the clock around a circle. The total feature count reaches 42, and the model grows to 178.6k parameters because of the wider input projection. Everything else -- optimiser, learning rate, gradient clipping, encoder length, early stopping, normalisation -- are identical to V1.

\subsection{Evaluation}

All variants is evaluated on the same held-out validation and test partitions using four metrics:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{MAE} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}|y_i - \hat{y}_i|, \qquad
\mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
R^2 = 1 - \frac{\displaystyle\sum(y_i-\hat{y}_i)^2}{\displaystyle\sum(y_i-\bar{y})^2}, \qquad
\mathrm{MAPE} = \frac{100}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\left|\frac{y_i-\hat{y}_i}{y_i+\epsilon}\right|
\end{equation}

MAPE deserves particular attention for residential load. Household consumption swings from near-zero overnight values to afternoon peaks, so a model that makes proportionally large errors on low-consumption periods will score bad on MAPE even if its absolute errors are modest. That makes MAPE a more honest measure of practical accuracy than MAE or RMSE alone for this dataset.

\subsection{V1 vs. V2: PCA Without Wavelets Gives Mixed Results}

V2 and V1 are close on aggregate error. V2 slightly improves RMSE and R\textsuperscript{2} (257.00 vs. 259.44, and 0.8675 vs. 0.8649), but MAE and MAPE get worse (80.33 vs. 79.19, and 28.93\% vs. 27.05\%). So PCA alone is not giving a clear net win on this dataset.

The likely reason is that this PCA step is close to a rotation, not a strong compression. We keep 12 components out of 14 continuous inputs, then append those components to the original feature set. That expands the input space while keeping substantial redundancy. The traces in Fig.~\ref{fig:notebook_traces} are therefore very much similar to V1.

\subsection{V1 vs. V3: What Happens When Optuna Picks Too Small a Model}

In this run, Optuna did almost nothing useful for predictive quality. V3 is slightly worse than V1 on all four metrics, and it also does not beat V2 on MAE, RMSE, and R\textsuperscript{2}. The long-window traces for V2 and V3 in Fig.~\ref{fig:notebook_traces} look nearly identical.

The selected setup (hidden size 16, one attention head, 89k parameters) likely underfit the full 14-meter dataset. This is a common failure mode with early stopping: smaller models can looks good in early epochs and then plateau.

The practical fix is to keep Bayesian search but enforce a capacity floor, for example hidden size $\geq 32$ and more than one attention head only.

\subsection{V1 vs. V4: A clean comparison}

V4 is directly comparable to V1 because both are using the same full dataset, meter count, training budget, and GPU setup. V4 improves all four test metrics over V1:

\begin{itemize}
  \item MAE: $79.19 \to 78.57$ (a 0.8\% drop)
  \item RMSE: $259.44 \to 257.02$ (a 0.9\% drop)
  \item R\textsuperscript{2}: $0.8649 \to 0.8674$ (+0.25 percentage points)
  \item MAPE: $27.05\% \to 23.58\%$ (a 12.8\% relative reduction)
\end{itemize}

The gain is not uniform across metrics. RMSE and R\textsuperscript{2} improve only slightly, while MAPE drops much more. So the final model is helping most on proportional error, especially during lower-load periods.

\subsection{V2 vs. V4: Did wavelets help enough?}

This is the most direct wavelet test, since both variants include PCA already. On test data, V2 and V4 is effectively tied on RMSE and R\textsuperscript{2}:

\begin{itemize}
  \item RMSE: $257.00$ (V2) vs. $257.02$ (V4)
  \item R\textsuperscript{2}: $0.8675$ (V2) vs. $0.8674$ (V4)
\end{itemize}

So if RMSE or R\textsuperscript{2} is the primary objective, wavelet features adds little over PCA in this experiment. The clear difference is MAPE (28.93\% in V2 vs. 23.58\% in V4), where V4 is substantially better.

This split result matches the notebook visuals in Fig.~\ref{fig:notebook_traces}: global shapes are very similar, while improvements are appearing mostly in smaller local deviations.

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}

The prior NITK study~\cite{reddy2022stacking} applied stacking ensembles (LSTM, Random Forest, DNN, Evolutionary Trees, combined by XGBoost) to a single industrial series. Their best ensemble reach R\textsuperscript{2} = 0.97--0.98 and RMSE around 636. Our V4 reaches R\textsuperscript{2} = 0.8674 on a much harder target.

The difference is in the data, not the model. Steel plant load follows a production schedule: it is smooth, predictable, and driven by process constraints. Residential load is driven by individual household decisions. Two meters in the same building can have very different profile depending on occupant behaviour. The MAPE range of 23--27\% in our results is not poor modelling, it reflects genuine unpredictability in the signal.

Structurally, TFT handles 14 meters in one pass through static categorical embeddings. SVR would need one model per meter seperately. TFT's attention weights also give a per-feature and per-timestep importance readout that SVR cannot provide. For multi-meter residential data these are practical advantages, not just theoretical ones.

\section{What Makes This Approach Different}

Several design choices makes this work different from the prior NITK study and from common energy forecasting practice.

\textbf{One model, many meters.} TFT trains a single model across all 14 residential meters using static meter-ID embeddings. This avoid the combinatorial cost of training and maintaining one model per entity which is a meaningful practical difference when the number of meters scales to hundreds.

\textbf{Wavelet statistics as an optional frequency view.} DWT sub-band features add frequency-domain context without changing TFT architecture. In our runs, this did not materially change RMSE/R\textsuperscript{2} over PCA-only inputs, but it was helping MAPE when combined with PCA under matched conditions.

\textbf{Leakage-free PCA.} The scaler and PCA are fitted on the training partition only. Some published studies fits PCA on the full dataset before splitting, which leaks validation and test distribution statistics into the training pipeline. Our implementation avoids this.

\textbf{Cyclical time encodings.} Hour of day as an integer from 0 to 23 creates a spurious distance of 23 between the last hour of one day and the first hour of the next. Sine and cosine encodings wraps this correctly. The improvement is small but costs nothing.

\section{Conclusion}

Four TFT variants were tested on the THC multi-meter residential dataset (144,256 rows, 14 meters, 30-minute intervals). The main findings are summarised below only.

V1 established the baseline at test R\textsuperscript{2} = 0.8649 and MAPE = 27.05\%. V2 (PCA) gave mixed results: slightly better RMSE/R\textsuperscript{2}, but worse MAE/MAPE. V3 (PCA + Optuna) reduced model size but did not improve predictive quality and was generally close to, or worse than, V2 and V1. V4 (wavelet + PCA), trained under the same conditions as V1, was improving all baseline metrics, with the strongest gain in MAPE.

Two practical takeaways matter most. First, wavelet features were not a universal win: they had little effect on RMSE/R\textsuperscript{2} compared with PCA-only inputs, but they helped percentage error. Second, Optuna search as configured here was not useful; capacity constraints in the search space needs tightening before drawing conclusions from HPO.
